I'm going to argue why I think math is the best tool we have to understand reality. And how I've come to believe its less perfect than I thought.

Here's what fascinated me. You start with a few things you are willing to believe are true. These are your axioms. You combine them in various ways you believe are logically valid, and out pops a new idea that must also be true. You repeat. Eventually you've got proofs for why the earth is a sphere and the speed of light is ~300,000 km/s.

If your axioms are actually correct, and you didn't make a logical error along the way, your conclusion _must_ be correct. You have almost no choice but to believe it. I find that guarantee so fascinating.

So math is perfect. Great, but, But the last paragraph already revealed its flaw.

You still have to assume your axioms. And here's the part I didn't appreciate before, you also have to assume the rules of logic themselves. "If A, and A implies B, then B" is itself something you're taking on faith.

This realization was triggered when I listened to Donald Hoffman [talk](https://youtu.be/reYdQYZ9Rj4?si=7sdDAC0xOH5DjR6c) for three hours about how nothing is real. At one point he criticized a colleague for assuming math itself is fundamental. I was a bit offended at first. I felt my beliefs being shattered. But after chewing on it, I understand his point. You still have to make assumptions, about both your axioms and your logic itself.

1+1=2 feels obvious. Modus ponens feels obvious. But "feels obvious" isn't a proof. You can't step outside logic to prove logic, because you'd need logic to do the proving. This feels adjacent to Gödel's incompleteness theorem, although I've learned about that only in passing, I might be misconstruing.

So math isn't the silver bullet I thought. It doesn't give you truths about reality. It gives you a conditional: _if_ you accept these axioms, and _if_ you accept these rules of logic, then everything downstream follows.

But I still think it's the best tool we have. And here's why.

The assumptions math asks you to swallow are the ones basically every human already agrees on. Nobody's really out there disputing that 1+1=2, or that a thing can't be both true and false at the same time. The foundations are shaky in theory, but in practice they're about as solid as anything gets. Math is what you get when you push your assumptions down as far as they'll go, and you land on a layer almost nobody disagrees with.

That's pretty good, I think.
